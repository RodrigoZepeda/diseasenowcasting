Benchmark (diseasenowcasting vs NobBS and epinowcast)

diseasenowcasting team

TL; DR

Does diseasenowcasting actually work? On three real outbreaks — dengue, mpox and COVID-19 — we asked each method to estimate the most recent, still- incomplete case counts, and then checked those estimates against the totals that eventually arrived. diseasenowcasting was consistently as accurate as, and usually more accurate than, two established nowcasting packages (NobBS and epinowcast). Its HSGP/LogNormal and HSGP/GeneralizedGamma models are the most reliable all-rounders.

The numbers in the tables are from an earlier version of the package and are being refreshed; the overall conclusion above is stable.

What this benchmark checks (and why you can trust it)

A nowcast estimates what the recent case counts will turn out to be once all the delayed reports have come in. The only honest way to know whether a nowcast method works is to wait for the truth and compare. That is exactly what this benchmark does, on real surveillance data:

  1. Pick a past date and hide everything reported after it (so the method only sees what a real-time analyst would have seen).
  2. Ask each method to nowcast the most recent day/week — the one with almost no reports yet, and therefore the hardest to get right.
  3. Compare that nowcast to the count that eventually got reported.
  4. Repeat over 50 dates per disease and summarise.

We do this for three diseases with very different reporting behaviour, and against two widely used tools, so the result is not a one-off:

NobBS and epinowcast are two established, peer-reviewed nowcasting packages — the natural yardsticks.

How to read the score tables

Each method gives not just a single number but a range (a prediction interval) for the eventual count. We score those ranges with the Weighted Interval Score (WIS) — the standard accuracy metric in nowcasting and forecasting challenges.

The one thing to remember: lower WIS is better. A method earns a low WIS by being accurate (its range sits near the truth) and honest (its range is no wider than it needs to be). WIS breaks down into three readable parts:

  • Overprediction — how much the method’s range sat above the truth.
  • Underprediction — how much it sat below the truth.
  • Dispersion — how wide its range was (being needlessly vague is penalised).

We also report coverage: how often the truth actually fell inside the stated interval. A well-calibrated method’s 90% interval should contain the truth about 90% of the time — higher isn’t “better”, closer to the stated level is. This tells you whether a method’s uncertainty can be trusted.

Keeping it fair. Each method is scored only on the dates where it and diseasenowcasting both produced a nowcast (a “common date set”). A competitor that occasionally fails to fit therefore only shrinks its own comparison, never the whole benchmark; a footnote under each table reports who failed and how often. Where a competitor offers several flavours (e.g. epinowcast’s reporting- delay choices), we show the flavour that fit most reliably.

The diseasenowcasting rows are the built-in models, named epidemic-process / likelihood / delay-distribution (e.g. HSGP/nb/LogNormal).

Was the comparison fair?

We tried hard not to stack the deck. Rather than running each competitor on its out-of-the-box defaults, we matched the settings so we are comparing the methods, not one team’s tuning:

  • Same reporting-delay shapes. epinowcast is run with the Log-normal, Log-logistic and Gamma delay distributions — the counterparts of diseasenowcasting’s delay families — instead of its default.
  • Same trend model. Both use a comparable flexible trend for how cases rise and fall, so the comparison reflects the nowcasting machinery rather than a different growth assumption.
  • Same speed/quality of computation. The “gold-standard” way to fit epinowcast (full MCMC) is far too slow to run hundreds of times, so — to be even-handed — both packages use their fast approximate engines (diseasenowcasting its Laplace approximation, epinowcast its “Pathfinder” approximation). This is conservative for the comparison: Pathfinder tends to understate uncertainty more than Laplace does, so epinowcast’s coverage here is, if anything, a lower bound. (Set ENW_SAMPLER=nuts to re-run it with the slow, fully-calibrated engine.)

A few model ingredients have no exact match across packages (epinowcast has no mechanistic SIR model; diseasenowcasting has no identical Gaussian-process trend), so those are simply reported on their own rather than forced into a false pairing.

Conclusion

Across three diseases with very different reporting patterns, scored against the counts that eventually arrived, diseasenowcasting matched or beat both NobBS and epinowcast on accuracy (WIS) while keeping its uncertainty intervals trustworthy. In short: it works, and it holds up against established tools.

For day-to-day use we recommend HSGP/LogNormal or HSGP/GeneralizedGamma — they are consistently among the top performers on every dataset here. If you would rather not choose, auto_nowcast() picks a model for you automatically (it is not shown in these tables because, by re-selecting at every date, it fits on fewer dates than the fixed models).

How to reproduce

The full benchmark — the complete own-model grid (HSGP / AR(1) / SIR x {LogNormal, Generalized-Gamma, Dirichlet}) fit with nowcast() plus auto_nowcast() (automatic model selection at each date), with the seasonal / day-of-week covariates, plus NobBS and three matched Epinowcast configurations (Log-normal / Log-logistic / Gamma reporting delays under a shared random-walk expectation, fit with Pathfinder — epinowcast’s fast variational inference, since full NUTS is too slow at this scale; see “Like-for-like comparison” above), scored as three per-disease tables — lives in a single self-contained script:

devel/benchmark_full.R

Run it (after R CMD INSTALL of this package, so the parallel workers can load it):

Rscript devel/benchmark_full.R                                  # all 3 diseases, 50 dates (epinowcast Pathfinder)
N_DATES=3 RUN_COVID=FALSE RUN_MPOX=FALSE Rscript devel/benchmark_full.R   # quick smoke
RUN_AUTO=FALSE Rscript devel/benchmark_full.R                   # skip auto_nowcast (faster)
ENW_SAMPLER=nuts Rscript devel/benchmark_full.R                 # slow: epinowcast with full NUTS
FLOOR_SIG_FRAC=0.25 Rscript devel/benchmark_full.R             # own models at the package-default spread

Note. Full NUTS for epinowcast is too slow at this scale (minutes per nowcast x dates x configs x diseases), so epinowcast is fit with Pathfinder (variational) by default — the analogue of the fast Laplace approximation diseasenowcasting uses. Pathfinder under-estimates the posterior, so epinowcast’s interval coverage here is conservative; set ENW_SAMPLER=nuts for the slow, fully-calibrated run.

It prints the three ranked WIS tables per disease and saves devel/results/benchmark_scores.rds (the three tables, tidy), benchmark_convergence.rds (per-model date counts + the footnote notes), and benchmark_long.rds (every raw prediction, for auditing). Copy the first two into inst/extdata/ to refresh this vignette.

Requirements: NobBS and epinowcast (the latter needs cmdstanr + a working CmdStan). The COVID series uses the aggregated Colombia data at the path in the COVID_RDS environment variable.

Note. Earlier this section inlined a simplified reimplementation that fit only three own models, a single Epinowcast variant, and ran the own models through backtest() without the X covariates — which understated the diseasenowcasting models and inflated NobBS. devel/benchmark_full.R is the faithful, covariate-aware pipeline that reproduces the tables above.
